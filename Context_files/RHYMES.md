# RHYMES DEFINED BY NATURE OF SIMILARITY

perfect rhyme, true rhyme, full rhyme – <br>
June/moon <br>

imperfect rhyme, slant rhyme, half rhyme, approximate rhyme, near rhyme, off rhyme, oblique rhyme – <br>
prayer/despair <br>
air/cigar <br>
astir/door <br>

augmented rhyme –  <br>
A sort of extension of slant rhyme. A rhyme in which the rhyme is extended by a consonant. <br>
bray/brave <br>
grow/sown <br>

diminished rhyme – <br>
This is the reverse of an augmented rhyme. <br>
brave/day <br>
blown/sow <br>
stained/rain <br>

unstressed rhyme – <br>
Rhymes which fall on the unstressed syllable <br>
very/sorry <br>
forgiven/hidden 
prison/heaven <br>

eye rhyme – <br>
These often reflect historical changes in pronunciation. Some poets (knowing that some of these older rhymes no longer rhyme) nevertheless continue to use them in the name of convention and convenience. <br>
day/eternity (See Above) <br>
among/along <br>
Queen/been <br>
prove/Love <br>

identical “rhyme” – <br>
Which really isn’t a rhyme but is used as such. <br>
"Pausing in Front of our Palsied Faces <br>
Time compassion took – <br>
Arks of Reprieve he offered us – <br>
Ararats – we took" <br>
partake/take <br>

rich rhyme – <br>
Words or syllables that are Homonyms. <br>
belief/leaf <br>

assonant rhyme – <br>
When only the vowel sounds rhyme. <br>
Eyes/Paradise <br>

consonant rhyme, para rhyme – <br>
When the consonants match. <br>
heal/hell <br>
hair/here <br>

feminine para rhyme – <br>
A two syllable para rhyme or consonant rhyme. <br>

scarce rhyme – <br>
May not be an altogether substantial category, as there is no difference between a scarce rhyme and any other rhyme except that the words being rhymed have few options. 
But, since academia is all about hair-splitting, I looked and looked and found these: <br>
guess/Rhinoceros (slant rhyme) <br>
Mortality/Fidelity (extended rhyme)<br>
Girls/Curls (true rhyme) <br>

macaronic rhyme – <br>
When words of different languages rhyme. <br>
see/me/Sabachthani (Google it if you’re curious.) <br>

trailing rhyme –  <br>
Where the first syllable of a two syllable word rhymes (or the first word of a two-word rhyme rhymes). <br>
ring/finger scout/doubter <br>

apocopated rhyme – <br>
The reverse of trailing rhyme. <br>
finger/ring doubter/scout. <br>

mosaique or composite rhyme – <br>
Rhymes constructed from more than one word.  <br>
(Astronomical/solemn or comical.) <br>


# RHYMES DEFINED BY RELATION TO STRESS PATTERN

one syllable rhyme, masculine rhyme – <br>
The most common rhyme, which occurs on the final stressed syllable and is essentially the same as true or perfect rhyme. <br>
shamed/blamed <br> 
out/doubt <br>

light rhyme – <br>
Rhyming a stressed syllable with a secondary stress <br>
chance/advance <br>
espy/try <br>
He/Poverty <br>

extra-syllable rhyme, triple rhyme, multiple rhyme, extended rhyme, feminine rhyme – <br>
Rhyming on multiple syllables. <br>
Mortality/Fidelity <br>
Immortality/Vitality <br>
Tremendousness/Boundlessness <br>
crucify/justify <br>

wrenched rhyme – <br>
Rhyming a stressed syllable with an unstressed syllable <br>
predistined/Land <br>
power/despair <br>


# RHYMES DEFINED BY POSITION IN THE LINE
end rhyme, terminal rhyme – <br>
All rhymes occuring at line ends: in other words, the standard procedure. <br>
chance/advance <br>
June/moon <br>

initial rhyme, head rhyme – <br>
Alliteration or other rhymes at the beginning of a line. <br>
To Stump, and Stack – and Stem – <br>
Too small – to fear – <br>
Too distant – to endear – <br>
Entombed by whom, for what offense <br>

internal rhyme – <br>
Rhyme within a line or passage, randomly or in some kind of pattern: <br>
It waits upon the Lawn, <br>
It shows the furthest Tree <br>
Upon the furthest Slope you know <br>
It almost speaks to you. <br>

leonine rhyme, medial rhyme – <br>
Rhyme at the caesura and line end within a single line. <br>

caesural rhyme, interlaced rhyme –  <br>
Rhymes that occur at the caesura and line end within a pair of lines: like an abab quatrain printed as two lines. <br>
"Sweet is the treading of wine, and sweet the feet of the dove; <br>
But a goodlier gift is thine than foam of the grapes or love. <br>
Yea, is not even Apollo, with hair and harp-string of gold, <br>
A bitter God to follow, a beautiful God to behold?" <br>

# Rhymes Defined By Position in the Stanza or Verse Paragraph

crossed rhyme, alternating rhyme, interlocking rhyme – <br>
Rhyming in an ABAB pattern. <br>
(Any of Emily Dickinson’s poems written in Common Meter would contain Crossed Rhymes.) 
<br>

intermittent rhyme – <br>
Rhyming every other line, as in the standard ballad quatrain: xaxa. <br>

envelope rhyme, inserted rhyme <br>
–  Rhyming ABBA. <br>

irregular rhyme –  <br>
Rhyming that follows no fixed pattern (as in the pseudopindaric or irregular ode). <br>
(Many of Emily Dickinson’s poems seem without a definite rhyme scheme but the admitted obscurity of her rhymes – such as ring/run in the poem "Because I could not stop for death" – serve to obfuscate the sense and sound of a regular rhyme scheme. In fact, and for the most part, nearly all of Dickinson’s poems are of the ABXB pattern – the pattern of Ballad Meter . This assertion, of course, allows for a wide & liberal definition of “rhyme”.) <br>

sporadic rhyme, occasional rhyme – <br>
Rhyming that occurs unpredictably in a poem with mostly unrhymed lines. Poem 312 appears to be such a poem. <br>

thorn line – <br>
An un-rhymed line in a generally rhymed passage. <br>

# RHYME ACROSS WORD BOUNDARIES

broken rhyme – Rhyme using more than one word:  <br>
thro’ it/do it <br>
Or rhyme in which one word is broken over the line end: <br>
"I caught this morning morning’s minion, king- <br>
Dom of daylight’s dauphin, dapple-dawn-drawn Falcon, in his riding <br>
Of the rolling level underneath him steady air, and striding <br>
High there, how he rung upon the rein of a wimpling wing…"

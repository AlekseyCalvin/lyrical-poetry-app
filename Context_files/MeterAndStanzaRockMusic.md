# **Meter and Stanza in Rock Music**

**A Historical Perspective**

**Aleksander Bjelčevič**

## **1 Introduction**

This article examines meters and stanzas in rock music, and the history of stanzaic forms used in rock music. The material of my investigation is Slovenian and British/American rock music, Slovenian, German, British/American literary poetry (such as Coleridge and Goethe), folk songs, and Latin and vernacular hymns. Students and lay persons usually believe that rock lyrics are written in free verse (probably because rock music is associated with modernity and thus with modern poetry). However, it is actually quite the opposite: the meter and stanza in rock are very traditional because rock music originates from folk song. Because of this, certain types of rock lyrics (blues rock, hard rock, metal, punk, and grunge) use the same type of stanza forms as folk song. Free verse (i. e., non-metrical verse) appears in rock when it is recited; that is, when it does not follow the rhythm of the music or where the rhythm of the music is not syllabotonic (e. g., rap music). This paper discusses metrical verse, not free verse.

I intend to show the following:

1. Meter and stanza in rock lyrics are usually metrical, and not free;  
2. Many stanzaic types used in rock songs (e. g., the long measure) formally originate from folk songs and from religious hymns, and therefore they have a traditional form;  
3. Many folksong stanzas are of medieval Latin origin; that is, they are derived from Latin church hymns. The stanzaic forms migrated from Latin to vernacular languages, from religious hymns to folk songs, and then to rock songs.  
4. The very same stanzas that are of church and folk origin also exist in literary poetry (e. g., Goethe, Heine, Byron, Keats, Ann Bradstreet etc.) and in children's songs as well. Vernacular religious hymns, folk song, children's songs, literary poetry, and pop and rock songs all have, to some extent, the same metrical form and the same stanzaic form. This also means that their form is (to some extent) international.  
5. Rock musicians are sometimes unaware of the type of stanzas they are using.

## **2 Theory**

### **2.1 Meter and versification systems**

From the textual point of view (not from the musical point of view), the lyrics of rock, folk, and church songs belong to the following three versification systems: syllabic, accentual, and syllabotonic.

a) Syllabic verse has a fixed number of syllables per line (e. g., eight), regardless of the number of stresses; this system is characteristic of French, Polish, and Italian poetry (to some extent), medieval Latin poetry, and most German and Slovenian religious hymns up to the seventeenth century;

b) Accentual verse or strict-stress meter has a fixed number of ictuses/stresses per line regardless of the number of syllables; that is, the number of unstressed syllables varies (usually between zero and two syllables), as in poems by Coleridge, in the Old English *Beowulf*, and so on. For example, each line in Shelley's poem "The Sensitive Plant" or Goethe's "Erlkönig" has four ictuses, whereas the number of unstressed syllables varies from zero to two syllables; the rhyme is masculine (the numbers 0, 1, and 2 in the schema below denote the number of weak (i. e., unstressed syllables):

The sensitive plant, like one forbid  
 U \-  U U  \-     U    \-   U  \-                1-2-1-1-

Wept, and the tears within each lid  
  \-   U   U   \-     U  \-   U    \-             0-2-1-1-

Of its folded leaves, which together grew,  
 U U U \-      U U U U U \-                     (Text notation verbatim from source)

Were changed to a blight of frozen glue.  
 U   \-       U  U   \-    U  \-  U   \-          1-1-2-1-  
(Shelley: "The Sensitive Plant")

c) Syllabotonic (or accentual-syllabic) verse fixes both the number of stresses and the number of syllables within each line. This has been the most common verse system in most Germanic and Slavic languages (Polish is an exception) in the last four hundred years. It is the most common in all branches of poetry: in rock music, in church hymns, and in literary poetry.

In songs such as church, folk, and rock songs, the rhythm of the lyrics is subordinated to the rhythm of the music or the beat, which means that rhythm is defined by musical stress rather than lexical stress. (In contrast, the majority of old Gregorian chants do not have a beat rhythm.) Two things can be deduced from this: (1) the same syllabotonic melody can be used to sing a song with syllabic or accentual rhythm, and (2) the history of stanzas is connected with the history of melodies.

I use the following symbols in this paper: / for iamb, T for trochee, A for amphibrach, D for dactyl, Ac for accentual verse; associated numbers refer to the number of syllables (e. g., T8 \= octosyllabic trochaic line); and capital letters stand for masculine (rhymed) endings and small letters for feminine endings. Thus T 8787 xAxA refers to a four-line stanza with alternating eight- and seven-syllable lines and odd lines having non-rhyming feminine endings. P stands for polymeter (various stanzas within a poem) and S for stichic verse.

### **2.2 Stanzas**

Many songs are written in stanzas (or strophes); that is, every stanza in the particular song is metrically exactly the same as any other stanza in that song. (Paragraphs with irregular number of lines, regardless of their rhythmical pattern, are not stanzas, but verse paragraphs and they are not the subject of my paper.) The reason for the repetition of stanzas comes from the history of stanzaic forms. Stanzas evolved from verse that was being sung; the repetition of the melody caused the repetition of the metrical form of the lyrics. The following is an example of the ballad stanza or common measure I 8686: four lines, the first and the third line have eight syllables, and the second and the fourth have six syllables:

Because I could not stop for Death,

He kindly stopped for me;

The carriage held but just ourselves

And Immortality.

We slowly drove, he knew no haste,

And I had put away

My labor, and my leisure too,

For his civility.

*(Emily Dickinson: "Because...")*

The following is the Ambrosian stanza. In English hymnals it is called the long measure, and it is made up of four eight-syllable iambic lines:

I had eight birds hatcht in one nest,

Four Cocks were there, and Hens the rest.

I nurst them up with pain and care,

No cost nor labour did I spare

*(Anne Bradstreet)*

Some stanzas are very complex. The following is an example of the sixteenth-century Slovenian protestant hymn "Tiga očenaša obilna zastopna izlaga" (An Explication of the Pater Noster) written in syllabic verse; the number of syllables per line and the rhyming pattern is 989887644448448 aabbbccddccxfff.

This is a syllabic meter, so please pay attention to the non-correspondence between the length of the lines and the rhymes (stressed syllables are underlined): in syllabotonic versification the difference between the lines with odd and even numbers of syllables corresponds to the difference between feminine and masculine rhymes: lines differing in one syllable in terms of length also differ in rhyme: one has a feminine rhyme and one has a masculine rhyme and, vice-versa, lines with an even (or odd) number of syllables have the same rhyme. However, this rule usually does not apply to Slovenian syllabic verse: two lines of the same length (e. g., eight-syllable lines) may have different rhymes (feminine or masculine) and, vice-versa, seven- and six-syllabic lines may have the same (e. g., feminine) rhyme. This means that stress is utterly ignored in Slovenian syllabic verse.

## **3 The history of rock stanzas**

When the Slovenian Romantic poet France Prešeren wrote the serenade "Pod oknom" ('Beneath the window') combining four-syllable and seven-syllable trochaic lines in a 447447 sequence:

Luna sije,               \-U-U      T4a  
kladvo bije              \-U-U      T4a  
trudne pozne ure že,     \-U-U-U-   T7B

this was not his invention, but was a form that he probably became acquainted with in church. This is an example of an old Slovenian church hymn:

Moja duša

vsak dan skuša

čast Marije spevati,

which is a translation of a twelfth-century Latin hymn:

Omni die

dic Mariae,

mea laudes anima;

The form is also known from the famous *Carmina Burana* poem "O Fortuna" (ca. AD 1300):

O fortuna.

velut luna

statu variabilis

Below I present some typical rock stanzas and their antecedents in children's, art, folk, and religious verse. The examples are presented in the following order: rock lyrics, art poetry (Slovenian, German, English), children's poetry, folk songs, vernacular church hymns, and Latin hymns.

Therefore it is one of the most widespread stanzas in children's poetry, folk poetry, and literary poetry, at least in Slovenian and German.

To better illustrate the character of this stanza, I suggest singing the lyrics of all the songs listed above to the melody of two songs listed (e. g., that of Nirvana and "Old McDonald").

Why do all these various songs (and you can think of another hundred similar ones) have the same stanzaic form? Is this merely a coincidence? Did Kurt Cobain of Nirvana compose a poem in four-line iambic stanzas by chance? And did Luther do the same by chance? And Goethe? And so on? Is it because they are geniuses that have an inborn rhythm that all the rest of us lack? Of course not: just as one does not write a sonnet by chance, so one does not write "Smells like Teen Spirit" in Ambrosian stanza by chance. Kurt Cobain, Luther, and Goethe became acquainted with this strophic form in preschool, in school, and in church.

Why is this not a coincidence? Because the theoretical possibility of choosing this type of stanza is approximately 1 in 500 quadrillion (500,000,000,000,000,000).

Let us make a calculation: trochaic lines have fifteen different lengths, iambic twelve different lengths, and amphibrachic and dactylic a little less. Namely, the possible lengths of trochaic lines are from two to sixteen syllables; two examples of a sixteen-syllable line are "Ve že noč, ki brídko sliši zdihovati me brez spanja, / ve že svítla zarja, dneva porodnica, de jo ljubim" (Prešeren, Gazela 3\) and "Abundantia peccatorum solet fratres conturbare; / Propter hoc Dominus noster voluit nos praemonere" (St. Augustine, Psalmus contra partem Donati). Lines can be combined into stanzas of two to sixteen lines in every possible way. If we disregard cultural and psychological factors, how many trochaic stanzas can we theoretically get? The calculation is the following: the number of T stanzas \= 15^2 \+ 15^3 \+ 15^4 \+ 15^5 \+ 15^6 \+ 15^7 \+ 15^8 \+ 15^9 \+ 15^10 \+ 15^11 \+ 15^12 \+ 15^13 \+ 15^14 \+ 15^15 \= 469 quadrillion \= 469,172,000,000,000,000. (It must be noted that this number is reduced significantly if, for example, sixteen-line stanza combinations are eliminated; however, the point remains the same.) The sum of iambic variations is less because iambic lines are no longer than thirteen syllables (at least not in Slovenian poetry): the number of I stanzas \= 15^2 \+ 15^3 \+ 15^4 \+ 15^5 \+ 15^6 \+ 15^7 \+ 15^8 \+ 15^9 \+ 15^10 \+ 15^11 \+ 15^12 \+ 15^13 \= 2 quadrillion \= 1,946,200,000,000,000. The sum of trochaic and iambic stanzas is 469,172,000,000,000,000 \+ 1,946,200,000,000,000 \= 471,118,200,000,000,000. How much is that? Let us say that a grain of wheat is one cubic millimeter. To store 500 quadrillion of grains of wheat, you need a room with a volume of half a cubic kilometer (1 × 1 × 0.5 km). That is the number of possible iambic and trochaic stanzas.

Yet, among all these possibilities, Kurt Cobain, Goethe, Anne Bradstreet, Luther, and so on chose exactly this form. They chose it more or less deliberately and consciously (less perhaps in the case of Kurt Cobain). The reason for this is tradition and cognition: it is much easier to grasp the 4 × 8 syllables of the Ambrosian stanza than the aforementioned 989887644448448 aabbbccddccxfff.

Of all these quadrillions, there are some hundred types of stanzas in use in western poetry; in Horst Frank's book of German stanzas (*Handbuch der deutschen Strophenformen*, Tübingen: Francke, 1993\) there are three hundred stanzas. (Almost all poets, at least the Slovenian ones, make up their own combinations in addition to the traditional forms, although they usually occur only once and perhaps with only one poet which is logical, taking into account the numbers presented above. This involves a sort of a stanza *hapax legomenon*.) In folk songs and rock lyrics the number is lower. In church songs the number is approximately 110 (as listed in the Metric List of Hymn Tunes for Hymnal 1982, Oremus Hymnal of the Episcopal Church, US; because the hymnal index does not distinguish between iambs and trochees, the actual number of stanzas is larger (approximately 150).

As we saw, rock lyrics use the same stanzaic forms as art poetry and church hymns. Thus, there is the contrast rock lyrics, once banned by priests (things have changed slightly with the emergence of Christian rock and contemporary Christian music) and, on the other hand, church hymns. Both have the same metrical structure and this sameness is no coincidence. There is a genetic connection between them. What connects these two extremes twentieth-century rock music and medieval Latin church hymns? The answer lies in the history of rock music.\[^1\] The immediate sources of rock music are various genres of folk music: gospel (i. e., folk religious music), blues, country, bluegrass, and so on. In turn, the metrical and stanzaic forms of folk music have two sources: ancient and medieval folk poetry, and Latin religious hymns. Thus the rock genealogy is the following:

Latin church hymns from the fourth century on ➞ Folk church hymns (+ secular Latin and non-Latin poetry of medieval goliards, Minnesänger, and old folk songs of various peoples) ➞ Recent secular folk songs (American blues, country, etc.) ➞ Rock songs

Latin songs were translated into vernacular languages. With this, the melodies and specific metrics of the Latin hymns were also introduced into folksongs.

When a melody of this type is used for a new song, a concrete form of stanza is also used with the lyrics. Then these same metrical and stanzaic forms spread to other branches of song: secular folk songs, children's songs, art songs, and rock songs.

So, these two genres, rock and church hymns, are connected via folk secular and religious music. Rock lyrics took its meters and stanzas from folk song, and folk song took its meters and stanzas from church hymns.

Let us return to stanzas.

## **4 How to determine the meter of a rock song**

Many rock lyrics are not isosyllabic and resemble the accentual meter with a variable 0 to 2-syllabic interval between the ictuses. Such lyrics are adjusted to the melody by pronouncing two syllables faster and substituting for the missing syllable by drawing out the previous one (a good example of this is the Howlin' Wolf song cited above). In order to determine the rhythm of a rock song, it does not suffice to analyze the written lyrics because it is also necessary to listen to the song. From the written viewpoint, the following Bob Dylan song is a combination of a four- and three-ictus accentual verse: "How many roads must a man walk down / Before they call him a man? / How many seas must a white dove sail / Before she sleeps in the sand?" The way Dylan actually sings it is an entirely different matter. Many rock lyrics are created independently of writing the music for them, which is why they are relatively free (see, e. g., the Jefferson Airplane and Grateful Dead lyrics). The basic question connected with analyzing the metrics of songs is what the correspondence between the lyrics and the musical rhythms is like and which musical rhythm is used.

Sometimes lyrics, although written in regular meter, appear on album covers as free verse. This means they have different line breaks. The ends of lines on paper do not match the ends of the lines when singing the song; rock singers write down the lyrics differently from how they actually sing them. Only an analysis of the sung verse can show the true metrical pattern of the song. The lyrics for "Paparazzo" by the Slovenian band Interceptor read as follows on the album cover:

hej ti

a ti sploh veš

da si največja gnida kar pozna jih svet

in faca tvoja res gnila je

saj tam kjer se pojaviš

vsakmu kri zavre

specialnost tvoja je oster nos

tiščiš ga tja kjer boš dobil čim večjo kost

za frende z denarjem ti vse bi dal

za dobro fotko ti še mamo bi prodal

*(Interceptor: "Paparazzo")*

There is no metrical pattern in this; it is free verse. However, if we listen to the singer's pauses, the picture is completely different; there is a completely regular rhythm and stanza: 15 4 12 5 4 12 5 4 12 5 4 12 xAAxBBxCCxDD, a four-time repetition of pattern 15 4 12 xAA:

## **5 Heterometric, logaoedic, and free verse**

Ambitious bands use more exotic metrics, including heterometric and logaoedic verse. Heterometric verse is a mixture of different rhythms (e. g., double and triple rhythms) within the same stanza:

Ko se ti splazim v misli                 U \- U \- U \- U           I7  
poklekni na tla                          U \- U U \-               A5  
ko ti pobožam lica                       U \- U \- U \- U           I7  
poljubi dlani                            U \- U U \-               A5  
ko te privijem k sebi                    U \- U \- U \- U           I7  
objemi noge                              U \- U U \-               A5  
priznaj u znoju sreče                    U \- U \- U \- U           I7  
da ti si moj bog                         U \- U U \-               A5  
(Slovenian band Demolition Group: "DAF")

Logaoedic verse is a mixture of double and triple rhythm within the same line (the mark "+" indicates a caesura, which is not a break but an obligatory word boundary within each line):

bila kot sonce ki sveti zame             U \- U \- U \+ U \- U \- U   I5 \+ I5  
si daleč vroča bleščeča                  U \- U \- U \+ U \- U       I5 \+ I3  
vse dneve svoje sem gledal vate          U \- U \- U \+ U \- U \- U   I5 \+ I5  
sežgala si mi oči                        U \- U \- U \+ U \-         I5 \+ I2  
(Demolition Group: "Dež")

**Scholarly Annotation on Logaoedic Verse:**

The I5 \+ I5 notation denotes two half-lines of Iambic pentasyllable separated by a strict medial caesura \+. This effectively "locks" the structural boundary, combining distinct metrical "chunks" into a single, cohesive logaoedic line.

**Free verse:** singing or reciting in only free verse is rare in rock music (due to my lack of musical background I find it difficult to assess Bob Dylan's songs, for instance). The Slovenian band Center za dehumanizacijo often recites its lyrics; the song "Umreti je treba pred jutrom" (You have to die before the morning comes) is a good example of syntagmatic free verse (1 verse \= 1 syntagm):

V snú

čín róbec

na kméčko krílo polágam

V studénec potápljam očí.

Sem káplja na kóži vabéči.

In déber,

ki pred tvojim korákom

se zóži.

V snú sem

Jézus brez kríža.

Ob tébi.

V snú sem kotaléča slíka

in slépi jék.

Rap lyrics mostly follow the same pattern: the lines consist of one sentence and end with a rhyme. The number of syllables is not important. The rhyme usually occurs in rhyming couplets (aabbcc...). The lyrics do not repeat the melody but are recited in a very rhythmic style. It seems at first glance that they only include trochaic or iambic verse of various lengths. However, it also seems that the rhythm is nonetheless merely syllabotonic and that it includes rhymed free verse of a syntactic type, where the ends of lines match the ends of sentences.

## **Conclusion**

This paper represents a simplified attempt to find the origin of the forms of stanzas used in rock music. A more detailed study will have to focus on at least three issues. First, only lyrics and no musical notation has been preserved from the majority of medieval songs; at the same time, many medieval lyrics are metrically unclear (it is not clear whether they followed the principle of syllabic length or the principle of stress), which is why it is difficult to determine whether the 8888 stanza, for example, is iambic, trochaic, or dactylic. Second, the same lyrics can be sung in several meters: in duple meter, triple meter, or a combination of both, which makes the song trochaic, dactylic, or combined. The last issue involves comparing the rhythm of modern-era songs with medieval rhythm (e. g., the rhythm of Gregorian chants), and interpreting medieval music notation, which in itself poses a great problem.

The origins of rock are generally well known. Rock music developed in the mid-1950s from American folk music popular from 1920 to 1950: gospel, blues, and to some extent ragtime (i. e., black American music) and white American country music, bluegrass, and urban protest folk music (e. g., Woody Guthrie). The 1920s blues and country music have the same origin: nineteenth-century black American music; the whites brought their folksongs from Europe, the blacks brought theirs from Africa, and by the end of the nineteenth century both traditions became very similar (especially the religious music of both was very similar). During the Great Depression, black Americans began moving from the south to the north (e. g., to Chicago and New York), where they replaced their acoustic guitars with electric ones (e. g., Muddy Waters and Howlin' Wolf). This led to the development of rhythm and blues and rock'n'roll. The later heavy metal and other genres also developed from blues, and rap developed from preachers' sermons at American Baptist and Methodist churches. In addition, English rock and folk-rock relied on the English folksong tradition; the case was similar with Yugoslav rock music, which drew on Serbian, Bosnian, and Macedonian folk music.
